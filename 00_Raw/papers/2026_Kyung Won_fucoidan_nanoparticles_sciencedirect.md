## Abstract

Naturally derived polysaccharides have great potential in biomedical applications due to their biocompatibility, biodegradability, and non-toxicity. In this study, we successfully synthesized nanoparticles of fucoidan, a sulfated polysaccharide derived from brown algae, via electron beam irradiation. This method enabled simple and efficient synthesis in aqueous solution without the need for chemical cross-linkers or toxic reagents. By adjusting the electron beam irradiation intensity and fucoidan concentration, the nanoparticle size could be tuned from 100 to 215 nm. Structural analysis (FT-IR, ¹H-NMR, XRD) confirmed that irradiation led to increased crystallinity and cross-linking. Additionally, rheological analysis suggested a mechanism by which electron beam irradiation induced simultaneous chain scission and cross-linking. Doxorubicin loading into the nanoparticles resulted in pH-sensitive drug release properties, demonstrating stability at physiological pH and accelerated release under acidic conditions. In cell and animal assays, the doxorubicin-loaded nanoparticles exhibited high cytotoxicity against EMT6 breast cancer cells and reduced tumor volume by more than four-fold in vivo. This study presents a green and scalable method for preparing fucoidan nanoparticles using electron beam irradiation; it demonstrates their potential applications in cancer therapy and drug delivery systems.

## 1. Introduction

Polysaccharides—long chains of sugar molecules derived from animals, plants, microorganisms, and algae—are integral to many biological processes (Benalaya et al., 2024; Jabeen & Atif, 2024). They have attracted increasing attention in medicine and pharmaceuticals due to their unique properties. These biopolymers are biodegradable, biocompatible, and non-toxic, making them ideal for biomedical applications (Tiwari et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020). Notable examples include chitosan, which has antibacterial, antitumor, and anti-inflammatory properties; fucoidan, recognized for its antitumor and immunomodulatory effects; and hyaluronic acid, widely used in tissue engineering and cancer treatment. Additionally, pectin has been explored for drug delivery and tissue engineering through three-dimensional printing (Abourehab et al., 2022). Despite their potential, current production processes for polysaccharide-based materials are complex. Such processes often involve multiple components, chemical cross-linkers, and organic solvents, in addition to extensive washing and purification steps (Huwait et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2022).

In nanomedicine, fucoidan—a sulfated polysaccharide extracted from brown algae—has been identified as a promising anticancer agent and drug carrier. Various methods have been explored for delivering drugs such as ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, and doxorubicin (DOX) using fucoidan. These methods include emulsion, self-assembly, polyelectrolyte complexation, coacervation, and ionic cross-linking (Huwait et al., 2022; Sahu et al., 2011). However, conventional approaches present substantial limitations, such as structural modification requirements, lengthy preparation times, reliance on toxic chemicals, and labor-intensive purification processes. These limitations restrict the feasibility of large-scale production and broader adoption of fucoidan-based systems in biomedical applications (DE SA et al., 2023; Lane et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2016).

Cancer incidence and mortality rates are on the rise due to westernized lifestyles and an aging global population. A report from the World Health Organization predicts more than a 70% increase in global cancer cases by 2050. Cancer cells disrupt organ structure and function by uncontrolled proliferation of normal cells. Current treatment modalities include surgery, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy (Anand et al., 2023).

Radiation technology offers a versatile and powerful approach to materials processing and has seen extensive use in the medical field. Ionizing radiation, such as X-rays and gamma rays, is commonly used for cancer treatment and diagnostic imaging (Donya et al., 2014; Thierry-Chef et al., 2022). In contrast, non-ionizing radiation, including ultraviolet and infrared light, is widely utilized in industrial and scientific contexts (Jaganathan et al., 2015). Radiation has also been used to modify polymers, enabling processes such as cross-linking, sterilization, and structural enhancement that are crucial for producing medical devices and biomaterials with improved physicochemical properties (Riveiro et al., 2018). Electron beam irradiation, a specific type of ionizing radiation, has emerged as an innovative method for synthesizing nanoparticles. Unlike chemical synthesis methods, electron beam irradiation eliminates the need for toxic reagents, achieves high purity, and supports scalable production (Gonzalez-Martinez et al., 2016). This technique is particularly well-suited for producing synthetic polymer hydrogels and has been applied in cross-linking, grafting, and nanoparticle formation (Abedini et al., 2014; Čubová & Čuba, 2020; Daneshian et al., 2021; Kadlubowski, 2014; Li et al., 2007; Li et al., 2006; Zhou et al., 2022).

In this study, we synthesized fucoidan nanoparticles using electron beam irradiation in aqueous solution, without the addition of chemical reagents. This method provides a simpler and more efficient alternative to conventional synthesis techniques. The resulting nanoparticles were subjected to chemical structure analysis and rheological characterization to understand the effects of irradiation. Furthermore, DOX—a widely used chemotherapeutic agent—was loaded into the nanoparticles to evaluate their efficacy for targeted cancer therapy. To our knowledge, this work represents the first successful production of fucoidan nanoparticles using electron beam irradiation. This research introduces a novel, green, and scalable method for nanoparticle synthesis and highlights its potential for advanced applications in cancer treatment and drug delivery systems.

## 2. Experimental (Methods)

### 2.1. Materials

All chemicals and solvents used in this study were of reagent grade and utilized without further purification. Fucoidan (90% purity), extracted from *Undaria pinnatifida*, was purchased from Haerim Fucoidan, Korea. HPLC-grade water was obtained from Duksan. DOX hydrochloride (DOX·HCl) was purchased from LC Laboratories, USA (catalog number D-1000). Dialysis was performed using Biotech CE Tubing (MWCO 3.5–5 kDa, code 131198), obtained from Hankook Spectrum, Korea.

### 2.2. Synthesis of fucoidan nanoparticles

Fucoidan nanoparticles were synthesized by dissolving fucoidan in high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC)-grade water at concentrations ranging from 1% to 30%. The solution was vortexed for 1 hour to ensure complete dissolution, then centrifuged at 2500 rpm for 30 minutes. The supernatant was then collected and transferred into 12-well plates (650 µL per well, n = 3 plates). Electron beam irradiation was subsequently applied at a beam energy of 5 MeV and a temperature of 25 °C.

### 2.3. Electron beam irradiation

Electron beam irradiation was carried out using an accelerator provided by EB Tech Co., Ltd. (Biomedical Manufacturing Technology Center). Irradiation doses were controlled by adjusting beam currents and conveyor speeds. Low doses (1, 3, and 5 kGy) were applied using 1.73 mA at a speed of 15 m/min; medium doses (10, 30, and 50 kGy) were applied using 4.56 mA at 4 m/min; and high doses (100, 200, and 300 kGy) were applied using 5.30 mA at 0.5 m/min.

### 2.4. Particle analysis

Dynamic light scattering (DLS) measurements were performed using a Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern Panalytical, UK) to determine the sizes of fucoidan nanoparticles. The analysis utilized a polystyrene latex refractive index (RI) of 1.590, an absorption value of 0.010, and water as the dispersant (RI = 1.330, viscosity = 0.8872 cP). Measurements were conducted at 25 °C using ZEN0040 cuvettes (Radiolab).

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was conducted using an HT 7700 instrument (Hitachi High-Tech, Japan). Samples were prepared by depositing the nanoparticles onto carbon film-supported copper grids (Sigma-Aldrich), followed by rinsing the grids three times with deionized water before imaging.

Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) was conducted using an SU8220 instrument (Hitachi High-Tech, Japan). Pt/Au coating was conducted for analysis. The surface component analysis of the sample was performed using an Energy Dispersive X-ray (EDX) spectrometer.

### 2.5. Structural analysis

Proton nuclear magnetic resonance (¹H-NMR) spectra were recorded using an AVANCE III HD 500 spectrometer (Bruker Corporation, USA) with deuterium oxide (D₂O) as the solvent at 25 °C.

For degradation analysis, hydrolyzed fucoidan samples were subjected to HPLC using an Alliance system (Waters Corporation, USA) equipped with a Sugar-Pak 1 column (10 µm, 6.5 × 300 mm). The mobile phase consisted of 0.01 M Ca-EDTA, delivered at a flow rate of 0.5 mL/min with the column maintained at 90 °C. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) was performed using a Frontier instrument (PerkinElmer, Inc., USA), and energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy was carried out using an SU8220 (Hitachi, Ltd., Japan).

X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) was performed using a NEXSA instrument (ThermoFisher Scientific, USA). Analysis was conducted in an ultra-high vacuum environment (UHV) better than 1.0 × 10⁻⁹ Torr, and the sample surface was surveyed from 0 to 1400 eV.

### 2.6. Crystallization analysis

X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis was conducted using an Empyrean system with a PIXcel3D detector (Malvern Panalytical). The operating conditions included an X-ray generator power of 4 kW, a Cu tube operating at 1.8 kW, and a scan range from 1.5° to 140° in 2Θ.

Thermal decomposition behavior was assessed by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) with a Q500 analyzer (Waters Corporation). The heating rate was set at 10 °C/min under a nitrogen purge to prevent oxidative degradation during analysis.

### 2.7. Rheological measurements

Rheological measurements were conducted using an HR-20 stress-controlled rheometer (TA Instruments, USA) equipped with a 60 mm parallel-plate geometry and a fixed gap of 0.6 mm. All measurements were performed at 25 °C on fucoidan solutions prepared both before and after electron beam irradiation over a range of concentrations. Oscillatory frequency sweeps were carried out from 0.1 to 10 rad/s at a constant stress amplitude of 1 Pa to ensure that all data were collected within the linear viscoelastic regime. Because rheological measurements at relatively high concentrations required large sample volumes, while the amount of irradiated fucoidan available for each condition was limited, each condition was measured on a single-loaded sample. Two to three frequency sweeps were then repeated on this sample to confirm instrumental stability, and the resulting data were interpreted in a primarily qualitative and semi-quantitative manner, focusing on dose-dependent trends in viscoelastic behavior rather than on formal statistical comparisons.

The dynamic moduli obtained from the measurements were used to derive viscosity-related quantities. The relative viscosity η_rel was defined as the ratio of the zero-shear viscosity of the sample η₀ to that of the solvent η_s. The specific viscosity η_sp = η_rel − 1 represents the increment of the viscosity arising from the dissolved polymer chains. The intrinsic viscosity was defined as [η] = lim_{c→0} η_sp/c, in the limit of vanishing polymer concentration c, representing the polymer contribution to viscosity in the dilute regime. Since direct measurements of [η] at very low concentrations were limited by instrumental sensitivity, [η] was estimated from experimental data obtained at several concentrations using the Kraemer equation, ln(η_rel)/c = [η] − k[η]²c, where k denotes the Kraemer constant (Kraemer, 1938).

In this study, the experimental data from the DOX release from fucoidan nanoparticles are fitted to the Korsmeyer-Peppas equation (Eq. 1):

> M_t / M_∞ = kt^n

Where M_t/M_∞ is the fractional drug release, t is the time (hr), k is the transport constant (hr⁻¹), and n is the transport exponent (dimensionless).

### 2.8. Gel permeation chromatography (GPC) analysis

Molecular weight characterization of fucoidan and its irradiated nanoparticles were conducted using an Alliance e2695 GPC system (Waters Corporation) equipped with a 2414 Refractive Index Detector. The chromatographic setup included a series of columns—Ultrahydrogel Linear, 500, 250, and 120 (Waters Corporation). The mobile phase was a 0.05 N NaNO₃ aqueous solution. Pullulans, a class of polysaccharides, were used as a calibration standard. Fucoidan and nanoparticles irradiated at doses of 1, 10, and 100 kGy were evaluated at 35 °C with a flow rate of 0.8 mL/min.

### 2.9. Cell cultures and animals

Mouse breast cancer EMT6 cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum and 1% penicillin-streptomycin. Cultures were maintained at 37 °C in a humidified incubator with 5% CO₂, in accordance with ATCC guidelines. All animal experiments were conducted in compliance with requirements established by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Kyungpook National University (approval numbers: 2014-0154, 2017-0096, and 2020-0079). Male BALB/c mice (5 weeks old, weighing 16–20 g) were sourced from Hyochang Science (Daegu, Korea). Animals were housed under controlled environmental conditions at 20–24 °C, with a 12-hour light/dark cycle; they received sufficient water and commercial feed.

### 2.10. Mouse tumor models

To establish the tumor model, EMT6 cells (5 × 10⁶ cells per mouse) were subcutaneously inoculated into the right flank of BALB/c mice. Tumor development was monitored; when tumors reached a diameter of 4–6 mm (typically 14 days post-injection), the mice were considered suitable for nanoparticle-based drug treatment studies.

## 3. Results and Discussion

### 3.1. Preparation of fucoidan nanoparticles by electron beam irradiation

This study utilized electron beam irradiation to convert fucoidan, a natural polysaccharide, into nanoparticles without the use of chemical cross-linkers. We hypothesized that this novel method could hold promise for biomedical applications, where even trace amounts of chemical additives can pose risks. A schematic representation of the synthesis process is shown in Fig. 1. Initially, the fucoidan solution was centrifuged to eliminate all impurities and insoluble material, then evenly distributed across a 12-well plate. Upon exposure to electron beam irradiation, radicals were generated within the fucoidan chains. The radiation began to decompose water, producing hydrated electrons (e⁻_aq) and hydroxyl radicals (OH•). These reactive species interacted with sulfate groups (R-O-SO₃⁻) in the fucoidan to form sulfuric acid radicals (SO₃⁻). The sulfuric acid radicals subsequently reacted with hydroxyl or alcohol groups on the fucoidan, oxidizing them to alkoxy radicals (RO•). These alkoxy radicals facilitated cross-linking within the fucoidan chains by forming ether bonds (R-O-R) (Al-Assaf et al., 2006a; Wach et al., 2022). However, the highly reactive oxygen species (including the alkoxy radical) also react with fucoidan molecules, producing degradation intermediates such as hydroperoxides. These intermediates further accelerate the degradation reaction, leading to chain scission via beta-scission (Text S1). Additionally, hydroxyl radicals formed through water degradation contributed to the partial breakdown of the fucoidan structure. Therefore, we hypothesize that cross-linking and degradation would occur simultaneously during irradiation (Hou et al., 2012).

The concentration of the aqueous fucoidan solution plays a critical role in determining the balance between intermolecular and intramolecular cross-linking. At lower concentrations, molecules are spaced further apart, favoring the formation of intramolecular cross-linking and discrete nanoparticles. To regulate particle size, we varied the electron beam irradiation dose from 1 to 300 kGy and adjusted fucoidan concentrations to 1%, 3%, 5%, and 10%. DLS was utilized to analyze nanoparticle size after irradiation. The results indicated that increasing the irradiation dose at a fixed concentration resulted in smaller nanoparticles. Similarly, decreasing the fucoidan concentration at a fixed dose led to smaller particle sizes (Fig. 2A) (Khan et al., 2020; Sapnar et al., 2012). For instance, in a 1% fucoidan solution, nanoparticles measured 157.6 ± 5 nm after 1 kGy and 97.0 ± 1 nm after 300 kGy irradiation. In a 5% solution, particle sizes were 175.6 ± 1.5 nm at 1 kGy and 109.3 ± 2.5 nm at 300 kGy. At 10% concentration, particles measured 215.3 ± 4.7 nm and 131.6 ± 4 nm, respectively, for the same dose range. These findings demonstrate that increased fucoidan concentration leads to larger nanoparticle sizes at constant irradiation doses, attributed to enhanced intermolecular cross-linking. Higher irradiation doses correspond to a higher density of radicals, which promotes cross-linking among both single and multiple polymer chains, resulting in larger particle sizes due to intermolecular binding and higher concentrations. Therefore, fucoidan concentration and electron beam irradiation dose can be adjusted to achieve the desired nanoparticle size (Grabowski, 2020; Hu et al., 2022; Jabłoński, 2021).

This size-controlling effect is likely due to the predominance of intermolecular cross-linking at high fucoidan concentrations. We observed that at concentrations of 20% or 30%, bulk gels were formed upon exposure to 100 kGy irradiation (Fig. S1). The resulting solutions were then purified using dialysis membranes with a molecular weight cut-off of 3.5 kDa in reverse osmosis (RO) water for 2 days. The dialysis water was changed twice daily to remove low-molecular-weight fragments and residual radicals. The solid nanoparticle samples were then collected via lyophilization. The yields ranged from 36.8% to 76.6%, depending on the irradiation dose. To optimize for the smallest nanoparticle size, we selected a 1% aqueous fucoidan concentration and tested irradiation doses of 1, 10, and 100 kGy. These particular doses represent substantial increments in electron beam current and conveyor speed, making them ideal for analyses of changes in nanoparticle size. TEM was used to visualize the nanoparticles (Fig. 2B–G). The average hydrodynamic diameters measured by DLS for nanoparticles produced at 1, 10, and 100 kGy were 155 nm, 125 nm, and 100 nm, respectively. The measured Polydispersity Index (PDI) for the fucoidan nanoparticles prepared by electron beam irradiation ranged narrowly from 0.239 to 0.261. This narrow PDI range is indicative of the particles possessing similar electrical repulsion and physical properties, which is crucial for ensuring their stability and preventing aggregation. Furthermore, this PDI range is considered an excellent level for pharmaceutical nanoparticle formulations, demonstrating the high uniformity of the synthesized particles (Fig. S2).

The TEM results showed trends and sizes similar to those observed via DLS. Furthermore, both techniques confirmed a consistent size reduction with increasing irradiation dose. TEM imaging revealed uniform, spherical nanoparticles in all irradiated samples. In contrast, no nanoparticles were observed in the non-irradiated control sample (Fig. S3).

### 3.2. Structural analysis of irradiated fucoidan nanoparticles

Fucoidans exhibit numerous bioactivities, such as anticoagulant, anti-inflammatory, anti-angiogenic, and anti-adhesive effects. The biological activities of fucoidans are largely dependent on their structural features. Therefore, it was essential to investigate whether the fucoidan structure underwent any changes under electron beam conditions. To address this, ¹H-NMR, FT-IR, HPLC, TGA, and XRD assays were performed on samples exposed to different irradiation doses.

In the ¹H-NMR analysis, the spectrum revealed a signal ranging from 1.4 to 1.2 ppm, which was attributed to the methyl protons of L-fucopyranose. Intense and narrow signals between 2.1 and 2.3 ppm were assigned to the CH₃ protons of O-acetyl groups. Signals observed between 5.64 and 3.5 ppm were primarily attributed to H of α-L-fucopyranose residues and CH protons of O-substituted carbons. Electron beam irradiation tends to degrade fucoidan structures depending on the dose. However, structural analysis showed that both irradiated (1, 10, and 100 kGy) and non-irradiated fucoidan samples retained their basic structure (Fig. 3A) (Lutfia et al., 2020; Mabate et al., 2023; Reys et al., 2016).

To evaluate thermal stability as a function of nanoparticle size, TGA was conducted to examine the decomposition behavior of synthesized nanoparticles (Fig. 3B). All TGA curves displayed an initial weight loss around 45 °C, indicating evaporation of residual moisture. This finding suggests a relatively low moisture content and high material stability. The TGA results indicated that higher irradiation doses led to slower degradation rates. The control (non-irradiated) and 1 kGy samples exhibited a similar pattern: 10% weight loss occurred at 95 °C without extensive structural changes. In contrast, the 10 kGy sample showed a 10% weight loss at 130 °C, and the 100 kGy sample exhibited a 10% loss at 115 °C. The latter showed greater overall weight loss, likely due to its smaller particle size, which resulted in more heat exposure at the surface and, consequently, more rapid thermal degradation. Furthermore, the sharp weight loss observed at 175 °C for the control and 1 kGy samples was attributed to degradation of the molecular backbone of fucoidan. The 10 and 100 kGy samples degraded more slowly, likely due to intramolecular cross-linking. Beyond 250 °C, decomposition was associated with the loss of sulfate groups and carbon residue breakdown. Unlike other samples, the high-dose irradiated material exhibited slow, steady decomposition without abrupt weight loss regions. If electron irradiation had merely broken fucoidan into low molecular weight chains, we would expect a decomposition profile similar to the non-irradiated sample. However, the opposite was observed—indicating that strong cross-linking in high-dose irradiated fucoidan resulted in enhanced thermal stability and slower degradation (Ciesla, 2017; del Mastro, 2022).

FT-IR spectral analysis was performed to identify potential structural changes in fucoidan nanoparticles after electron beam irradiation (Fig. 3C). FT-IR spectra were recorded across the range of 4000 to 400 cm⁻¹ for different irradiation doses. In this analysis, the labels b (bending), ν (vibrations), s (symmetric), and as (asymmetric) refer to specific vibration modes (Lakshmanan et al., 2022). The IR spectra showed several characteristic peaks indicating the presence of functional groups and molecular vibrations: a broad absorption peak at 3397 cm⁻¹ corresponding to hydroxyl (O–H) groups; a peak at 1635 cm⁻¹ attributed to the carbonyl stretching vibration of acetyl groups (ν(C=O)); a peak at 1214 cm⁻¹ corresponding to the asymmetric stretching of sulfate groups (ν(O=S=O)); a strong peak at 1032 cm⁻¹ associated with glycosidic linkage vibrations (ν(C–O–C)); a peak at 830 cm⁻¹ due to bending vibrations of C–O–S groups; and a peak at 575 cm⁻¹ reflecting symmetric and asymmetric sulfate group vibrations (b_s(O=S=O)). Additionally, peaks observed in the range of approximately 1900–2500 cm⁻¹ were attributed to C–H stretching vibrations associated with pyranose rings found in fucose and galactose units (Palanisamy et al., 2017; Ptak et al., 2021).

XPS analysis indicated an increase in ether linkages in the C1s region of both fucoidan and the fucoidan nanoparticles, with a slight increase also observed in the O1s region. However, a decrease in the S2p signal was observed due to the electron beam, which was interpreted as the degradation or scission of the sulfate groups or the fucoidan backbone itself (Fig. S4).

HPLC polysaccharide analysis was conducted to assess potential changes in glycosidic bonding caused by electron beam irradiation. When comparing chromatograms before and after irradiation, the peak corresponding to nanoparticles appeared sharper and more uniform than that of the original fucoidan (Zhao et al., 2021). This observation is due to a difference in molecular weight distribution: chain-like polymers exhibit broader peaks because their molecular weights considerably vary, causing slower movement through the mobile phase. In contrast, nanoparticles exhibit sharper, more defined peaks due to their relatively uniform size and quicker migration through the mobile phase to the detection system. Furthermore, the analysis indicated that glycosidic bonds remained intact. No additional peaks associated with glycosidic bond cleavage or degradation products were observed (Fig. S5) (Zayed et al., 2020).

XRD analysis was also performed to determine the crystallinity of the fucoidan nanoparticles. The native, unprocessed fucoidan exhibited a disordered structure with no clear diffraction peaks. However, after electron beam irradiation, distinct peaks emerged in the XRD pattern of the nanoparticles (Mission et al., 2019). This finding suggests that irradiation induced partial crystallinity in the previously amorphous fucoidan (Fig. S6).

Additionally, SEM analysis, which provided images of the fucoidan particles before and after the electron beam treatment, confirmed the presence of spherical particles (Fig. S7). Normal fucoidans exhibit a relatively smooth and hard block form on the particle surface, with low porosity and robust structure aside from surface cracks. For fucoidan nanoparticles, the decomposition and cross-linking of the fucoidan chains work in combination, leading to their reconstruction into very rough and highly porous structures.

Elemental analysis was conducted to determine how the chemical composition of fucoidan was affected by varying intensities of electron beam treatment (Fig. 3D). The results showed an overall decrease in the elemental content of irradiated samples compared with non-irradiated controls. This decrease supports the conclusion that both decomposition and cross-linking processes were simultaneously induced by electron beam irradiation (Manas & Manas, 2012). The carbon content remained relatively unchanged, suggesting structural stability in the carbon backbone of fucoidan despite exposure to irradiation. Conversely, the oxygen content increased, likely due to oxidative reactions or the formation of additional cross-links during irradiation. The quantitative degree of sulfation (DOS) was confirmed to decrease as the electron beam irradiation dose increased. Quantitatively, the DOS was measured to be 10.32%, 9.76%, 9.09%, and 8.93% for the un-irradiated sample and samples irradiated at 1, 10, and 100 kGy, respectively. The occurrence of desulfation was further corroborated by a noticeable decline in sulfur content observed via Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDX) analysis (Fig. S8), resulting from direct decomposition or electron beam-induced cleavage.

Additionally, the UV spectrum of fucoidan nanoparticles prepared at different concentrations was analyzed (Fig. S9). Absorption measurements were performed to evaluate the optical and intermolecular interactions related to changes in nanoparticle size. Fucoidan has a linear or branched polysaccharide structure with a high molecular weight, yet it is independently dispersed. As a result, electron transitions and UV absorption in individual molecular units are typically low. However, as nanoparticle size decreases, intermolecular interactions become stronger. This leads to the proximity of π-electron clouds between molecules and broader absorption peaks. Optically, the absorption rate increases as more molecules interact with light, which is enhanced by the increase in surface area resulting from smaller particle sizes (He et al., 2024). Thus, the absorption of nanoparticles increases as the particle size decreases with increasing electron beam irradiation (Choi et al., 2014). Accordingly, differences in absorption arise from structural changes. If fucoidan were degraded into low molecular weight fragments by decomposition rather than forming cross-linked nanoparticles, the UV absorption would resemble that of conventional fucoidan.

According to the ¹H-NMR and HPLC results, the chemical structure of fucoidan and the synthesized nanoparticles remained unchanged after electron beam irradiation. FTIR analysis indicated a strong C–O–C bond at 1032 cm⁻¹ in the 100 kGy-irradiated sample, confirming cross-linking via radical bonding. TGA showed that the initial pyrolysis at 100 kGy was higher, and as nanoparticle size decreased, the surface pyrolysis temperature increased. Even at high temperatures, no specific point was identified at which the core structure decomposed due to cross-linking. XRD analysis confirmed that before irradiation, fucoidan remained amorphous, whereas after irradiation, crystalline nanoparticles formed through cross-linking.

### 3.3. Rheological and GPC analysis of irradiated fucoidan nanoparticles

Rheological analysis provided qualitative and semi-quantitative insight into how electron beam irradiation affects the solution viscosity of fucoidan and, indirectly, the conditions for nanoparticle formation. Because viscosity is sensitive to polymer chain dynamics and interchain interactions, irradiation-induced changes in viscosity can reflect the relative contributions of radical-induced chain scission and cross-linking.

The rheological behavior of fucoidan solutions was evaluated by dynamic frequency-sweep tests at concentrations of 1–30 % (w/v) (Fig. 4A). Within this frequency window, the solutions behaved as weakly viscoelastic fluids close to Newtonian fluids. The zero-shear viscosity η₀ was estimated from the low-frequency relation G″ ≈ η₀ω, and the specific viscosity η_sp was then plotted as a function of the volume fraction ϕ of fucoidan (Fig. 4B), assuming a density of 1 g/mL for fucoidan (Horinaka et al., 2020). At low concentrations, η_sp increased linearly with a slope of approximately 1, corresponding to the dilute regime. Above about 10%, the estimated slope increased to about 3.6, indicating a transition to the concentrated regime in which polymer chains overlap and entangle. For polymer solutions, the scaling exponent of η_sp with ϕ is 14/3 for θ-solvents and 3.9 for good solvents (Rubinstein & Colby, 2003). Although the limited number of data points restricts precise determination of the exponent, the observed slope is closer to the prediction for a good solvent. A previous study on fucoidan reported a scaling exponent of about 4.0, which further supports this interpretation (Citkowaka et al., 2019).

The effect of electron beam irradiation on the specific viscosity of fucoidan solutions was investigated at concentrations of 1–30 % (w/v) and doses of 1–100 kGy (Fig. 4C). To compensate for solvent evaporation during exposure, each sample was reconstituted to its original volume before measurements. At low concentrations, η_sp decreased monotonically with increasing dose, consistent with a reduction in hydrodynamic radius mainly due to chain scission. In concentrated solutions, η_sp instead exhibited a non-monotonic trend, increasing at low doses and then decreasing at higher doses. This temporary increase in viscosity may reflect limited interchain association or cross-linking, which leads to the formation of larger molecular structures and a transient increase in the hydrodynamic radius. At higher doses, chain scission becomes more prominent, breaking down these structures and thereby reducing the solution viscosity. These results indicate that irradiation induces both chain scission and limited cross-linking, and that their relative contributions depend on concentration and dose.

A substantial reduction in M_w was observed with increasing irradiation dose, from 183,000 g/mol in the unirradiated sample to 19,000 g/mol at 100 kGy, i.e., to roughly one-tenth of the original value. The simultaneous decrease in PDI from 2.05 to 1.42 indicates a narrowing of the molecular-weight distribution, suggesting that chain scission proceeds in a relatively uniform manner rather than through highly heterogeneous degradation. This narrowing of the distribution is consistent with the tendency of nanoparticles prepared at higher doses to exhibit narrower size distributions. Although the absolute values of M_n and M_w depend on the calibration standards and elution behavior, the observed trends in molecular weights and PDI qualitatively reflect the structural changes in fucoidan induced by irradiation.

Intrinsic viscosity [η] was analyzed to evaluate how electron beam irradiation affects the fucoidan chain structure, particularly the balance between chain scission and potential interchain cross-linking. As described in Section 2.7, [η] was determined using the Kraemer equation by fitting η_rel over a range of low concentrations. Fucoidan solutions at 1% (w/v) were irradiated at doses from 1 to 100 kGy, freeze-dried, redissolved in water at concentrations of 1.0–2.5% (corresponding to 1.0–2.5 g/dL for use in the Kraemer plots), and subsequently analyzed by rheological measurements (Fig. 4E). The intrinsic viscosity decreased gradually with increasing dose, consistent with the interpretation that chain scission reduces both molecular weight and hydrodynamic volume.

To further analyze the effects of irradiation on polymer structure, a semi-empirical model was employed to relate intrinsic viscosity [η], irradiation dose D, and the ratio of chain scission to cross-linking ρ (Kilb, 1959). The model is expressed as:

> [η]/[η]₀ = (1 − γρ/4)^α · ψ(γ, α)   (Eq. 2)
>
> D/D_max = γ(ρ − 4) / [(μ⁻¹ − 1)(4 − γρ)]   (Eq. 3)

where [η]₀ is the intrinsic viscosity before irradiation, μ denotes the fraction of the initial weight-average molecular weight remaining after irradiation to the maximum dose D_max, γ is a branching parameter, and α is the Mark-Houwink exponent. Based on the GPC data, μ at D_max was estimated to be approximately 0.104. For fucoidan in water we used α = 0.75, corresponding to good-solvent conditions. The correction factor ψ(γ, α) was estimated by fitting a smooth curve to the discrete values reported in Kilb's original work, giving:

> ψ(γ, 0.75) = 0.95 + 0.046 exp(3.94γ)

As a reference limit, we also considered the case of pure chain scission (ρ = ∞), in which the weight-average molecular weight decays exponentially with dose as M_w(D) = M_{w,0} exp(−βD) where β represents the number of chain scission per unit dose and M_{w,0} = M_w(0). Applying the Mark–Houwink relation (Rubinstein & Colby, 2003), the normalized intrinsic viscosity then follows [η]/[η]₀ = [M_w(D)/M_{w,0}]^α = exp(−αβD). This can be expressed in terms of μ as:

> [η]/[η]₀ = μ^{αD/D_max}   (Eq. 4)

Fig. 4F compares the experimental data with the model predictions. The black curve represents the fit of Eq. (2) to the experimental data, yielding ρ = 4.3. This indicates that, under the present irradiation conditions, chain scission events occur several times more frequently than cross-linking events, i.e., the degradation is clearly scission-dominated. The red curve corresponds to the theoretical limit of pure chain scission given by Eq. (4). The deviation of the experimental data from this pure-scission limit indicates that additional processes beyond chain scission are present. We interpret these additional contributions in terms of limited interchain association or cross-linking, in line with the non-monotonic viscosity changes observed at higher concentrations. The Kilb analysis is used here in a semi-quantitative way as a reference for the pure-scission limit, supporting the view that electron beam irradiation of fucoidan solutions leads to scission-dominated degradation accompanied by a limited degree of interchain association or cross-linking, rather than serving as definitive proof of macroscopic network formation.

### 3.4. In vitro study of irradiated fucoidan nanoparticles

We synthesized fucoidan nanoparticles with uniform size (approximately 100 nm), using the highest irradiation dose of 100 kGy to achieve the smallest particle size. To investigate the nanoparticle stability and binding properties with doxorubicin (a cationic drug), the zeta potentials of fucoidan nanoparticles manufactured using radiation doses of 1, 10, and 100 kGy were measured at −19.4, −18.7, and −15.9 mV, respectively (Fig. S10). The consistently negative zeta potential confirms that the positively charged doxorubicin can successfully bind to the fucoidan nanoparticles via electrostatic interaction.

Consequently, DOX was loaded onto the nanoparticles to serve as a chemotherapeutic agent. The binding of DOX to fucoidan occurred through electrostatic interaction between fucoidan's sulfate groups and DOX's amine groups, along with hydrophobic interaction between the anthracycline ring of DOX and hydrophobic regions of fucoidan. At room temperature, DOX was loaded onto both the control fucoidan and fucoidan nanoparticles by stirring in water for one hour. The drug loading efficiencies were measured to be 97.3 ± 0.19% for fucoidan nanoparticles and 72.4 ± 0.33% for the fucoidan control (Fig. S11). The higher loading efficiency of the nanoparticles is attributed to their high surface area-to-volume ratio, which enhances the number of available interaction sites for drug molecules and improves loading capacity.

The in vitro drug release profiles of DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles were evaluated at 37 °C under two pH conditions—pH 7.4 (physiological) and pH 5.5 (tumor microenvironment)—to assess pH-responsive drug release behavior (Figs. 5A, S12). Both drug-loaded formulations (fucoidan nanoparticles and control fucoidan) exhibited similar release patterns. At pH 7.4, more than 20% of the drug was released from both carriers within the first hour. After this initial release, a sustained drug release was observed; cumulative release reached 35.7 ± 0.8% for the nanoparticles and 33.4 ± 0.4% for the control at 48 hours. At pH 5.5, drug release was substantially higher. Within the first hour, DOX release from the nanoparticles and control fucoidan reached 34.5 ± 0.7% and 46.3 ± 0.3%, respectively. These results confirm that DOX release is pH-sensitive, with greater drug release under acidic conditions typical of tumor environments. The slower release at neutral pH helps reduce side effects and enhances drug accumulation in cancer cells, supporting the therapeutic potential of fucoidan nanoparticles (Mabate et al., 2023). The release rates accelerated as the pH decreased from 7.4 to 5.5, demonstrating that the medium's pH strongly affected the release behavior of DOX. The cumulative release of DOX over 48 hours from fucoidan nanoparticles and the fucoidan control was 45.2 ± 0.15% and 60.8 ± 0.7%, respectively. The release of a smaller amount of DOX from the nanoparticles supports their potential for sustained, long-term treatment.

The drug release mechanism was explored by fitting the experimental data to the Korsmeyer-Peppas model (Fig. S13). Among the four experimental groups, the Dox@Fuco-NPs (pH 7.4) formulation exhibited a significantly higher coefficient of determination (R² = 0.93) compared to the others, which showed poor correlations (R² ≤ 0.75). Consequently, the Korsmeyer-Peppas model was applied exclusively to the pH 7.4 group, yielding a release exponent (n) of 0.23 and a rate constant (k) of 0.18. The comparison between the experimental data and the model predictions reveals that the actual release profile displays a more pronounced initial burst than predicted (Fig. S13). This discrepancy likely explains why the calculated n value falls below the theoretical threshold for spherical nanoparticles (0.43). Therefore, we conclude that the release mechanism—while primarily driven by diffusion—is strongly influenced by the initial burst effect of surface-adsorbed drugs and the polydispersity of the nanoparticles.

The cytotoxicity of free DOX and DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles was evaluated using an MTT assay across 12 cell lines—10 cancerous and 2 normal. The results indicated that several cell lines showed less than 50% viability after treatment: C6 (36.8 ± 2.2%), PANC-1 (26.8 ± 0.7%), U87MG (48.4 ± 2.5%), LNCaP (22.5 ± 3.2%), EMT6 (15.8 ± 2.4%), HEK293 (13.6 ± 1.2%), and B16F10 (28.1 ± 2.2%) (Fig. 5B). Compared with free DOX, the DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles showed reduced toxicity in some cell lines, including 4T1, BNL CL.2, C6, NIH3T6.7, and PANC-1. However, cytotoxicity against MDA-MB-231 and B16F10 substantially increased, by 45.7% and 81%, respectively (Al-Assaf et al., 2006b; del Mastro, 2022; Reys et al., 2016). This enhancement is attributed to overexpression of P-selectin in these cell lines, which promoted greater uptake of the fucoidan-targeted nanoparticles. In the case of EMT6, HEK293, and B16F10 cells, viability dropped to less than 10% (Fig. 5B). Among these, EMT6 cells were selected for in vivo studies. EMT6 is widely used as a model for triple-negative breast cancer, a challenging subtype that lacks common therapeutic targets (HER2, ER, PR) and typically relies on chemotherapy, making it ideal for evaluating new treatments. Additionally, in vitro results showed that DOX and DOX-loaded nanoparticles had the most robust drug effects.

### 3.5. In vivo study of irradiated fucoidan nanoparticles

Finally, the therapeutic efficacy of DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles was assessed in an EMT6 breast tumor model using male BALB/c mice. EMT6 tumors were established by subcutaneously injecting tumor cells into 5-week-old mice. After 14 days of tumor growth, DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles were intravenously administered. The treatment group received the nanoparticle formulation, whereas the control group received phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Tumor volume and body weight were monitored over 24 days (Fig. 6A).

Treatment efficacy was assessed by measuring tumor volume and conducting histological analysis of tumor tissue. Mice were treated with DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles or free DOX (200 mg) every 3 days after the tumors reached approximately 50 mm³ in size. PBS served as the negative control (Fig. 6B). After 24 days, tumor volumes were as follows: control group (PBS), 4552.62 ± 511.27 mm³; free DOX group, 3062.61 ± 507.44 mm³; and DOX-loaded nanoparticle group, 1132.06 ± 282.68 mm³ (n = 3). Mice treated with the DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles exhibited significantly reduced tumor volumes—4 times smaller than the control group and 2.7 times smaller than the free DOX group (Fig. 6C). Across all groups, body weight remained stable without substantial changes during the treatment period, indicating minimal systemic toxicity (Fig. 6D).

## 4. Conclusion

This study presents a novel and green approach for synthesizing fucoidan nanoparticles using electron beam irradiation in aqueous solution, eliminating the need for chemical cross-linkers and organic solvents typically required in conventional nanoparticle synthesis methods. The technique successfully induced simultaneous chain scission and cross-linking, producing structurally stable nanoparticles with precisely tunable sizes. By varying fucoidan concentrations and irradiation intensities, nanoparticle sizes were tuned within the 100–215 nm range, allowing for customized physical properties.

Structural characterization via ¹H-NMR, FT-IR, XRD, and TGA confirmed the retention of core structural integrity, improved crystallinity, and high thermal stability due to strong cross-linked networks formed during irradiation. Rheological analysis demonstrated that electron beam irradiation substantially affected the viscosity of fucoidan solutions. At low concentrations, chain scission dominated, leading to a decrease in viscosity. At higher concentrations, cross-linking prevailed, thereby increasing viscosity. These dual behaviors supported a better understanding of nanoparticle formation mechanisms.

DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles exhibited superior drug loading efficiency (97.3%) compared with conventional fucoidan. Moreover, DOX showed pH-sensitive release behavior: at physiological pH (7.4), drug release was slow, reducing potential side effects; at acidic tumor pH (5.5), drug release accelerated, delivering higher concentrations to tumor cells. The DOX release mechanism from the nanoparticle carriers appeared to be strongly influenced by the initial burst effect as analyzed by the Korsmeyer-Peppas model.

In vitro assays confirmed strong cytotoxic effects across multiple cancer cell lines, with enhanced efficacy against triple-negative breast cancer models such as EMT6. In vivo, DOX-loaded fucoidan nanoparticles demonstrated excellent tumor suppression, reducing tumor volume by 4-fold versus control and 2.7-fold relative to free DOX, without observable side effects.

In summary, this research established a scalable, eco-friendly method for producing bioactive nanoparticles; it emphasizes their promising applications in targeted cancer therapy and advanced drug delivery systems. Future studies may apply this technology to other polysaccharides and therapeutic agents to further expand their biological applications and help to solve various biomedical problems.
